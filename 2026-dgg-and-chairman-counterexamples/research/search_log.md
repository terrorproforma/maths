# Research log and discarded approaches

This is a concise, shareable record of the exploration that led to the finite certificates. It records mathematical ideas and failure modes, not private chain-of-thought.

## Exact test used throughout

For a fixed flow instance, let `U+(x)` be the unsplittable routings with `y<=x+D`. A cost counterexample exists exactly when `x` is strictly separated from `conv U+(x)` by a nonnegative arc-cost vector. The finite separation program is

```text
maximize delta
subject to c·(y-x) >= delta   for every y in U+(x),
           c >= 0,
           sum_a c_a = 1.
```

A positive optimum is a checkable certificate. Restricted path lists are unsafe; every actual graph path must be enumerated or generated by an exact pricing oracle.

## Failed abstract cube constructions

The first promising family assigned one shared resource to each binary choice vector. As an abstract partition-constrained selection problem, every integral choice overloaded its corresponding resource, and removing one resource left a unique capacity-good but expensive choice.

The graph realization failed because paths are closed under prefix/suffix splicing. Once two intended paths merge and later split, the graph contains hybrid paths. Complete path enumeration restored cheap capacity-good routings and drove the separation value to zero.

### Lesson

Do not design a set system and then hope to realize it as graph paths. Design a splice-closed path language from the start, or use a graph in which the full path set is visibly finite.

## Off-support cost penalties

A tempting repair was to put cost only on hybrid or bypass arcs outside the support of the fractional flow. This cannot work. Applying the Dinitz–Garg–Goemans theorem to the positive-flow support gives an upper-good unsplittable routing entirely in that support, hence of zero additional cost.

### Lesson

A genuine cost obstruction must use costs on arcs already carrying fractional flow.

## Equal-demand and divisibility barriers

For equal demands, scale to unit demands, round each arc capacity up, and use integral min-cost flow. This gives an unsplittable flow of no greater cost within additive one demand. More generally, known positive results cover divisible demand chains.

### Lesson

Unequal, non-divisible demands are essential.

## Track and token-routing attempts

Layered merge–split networks were explored as finite-state path systems. A capacity-enforced token interpretation was useful, but three tracks cannot force one terminal per track under an additive-`D` allowance: if three demands use three gates, requiring even the two smallest demands to overload every gate leads, after summation, to the impossible inequality `2D<d2+d3`.

Four-track “one-hole” braids avoid this counting obstruction, but small symmetric templates tested at the configuration-polytope level remained decomposable.

### Lesson

Capacity can control hybrids by turning them into matchings, but small symmetric matching layers are too integral.

## Circular-interval and conflict-graph abstraction

The decisive abstraction was to stop trying to make every integral routing bad. It suffices that all upper-good routings lie on one side of a valid integral-hull inequality. Small weighted circular-interval experiments produced a triangle conflict pattern: every pair of cheap choices is forbidden, but the fractional cheap marginals sum to more than one.

The key realization was that this triangle can be implemented without hybrid paths by three nested cheap routes on a directed backbone. The third terminal uses the first backbone arc on both its cheap and expensive choices, allowing the three pair conflicts to live on `su`, `uv`, and `vw`.

## Final flow construction

The rational probabilities `(1/3,2/5,1/3)` and demands `(15,10,15)` make all three pair conflicts miss their additive bounds by exactly one. Setting all expensive integral choices to cost 30 gives the exact `58<60` separation.

The included script `search_dgg_family.py` reconstructs the symmetric parameter region and the `9/8` planar lower-bound supremum.

## Chairman search

The chairman attack used an inverse forcing-gadget design rather than brute-force matrix search. A decision column `J` deposits `p` discrepancy on a global accumulator when forced to a fresh row `A`. Two detector columns `K,L` make every assignment impossible if `J` is not sent to `A`.

The ideal parameter region is

```text
p < 1/4,
q > 1/2,
p+q < 3/4.
```

Five repetitions also require `p>1/5`. The rational choice `p=5/24`, `q=25/48`, together with off-support weight `eta=1/1000`, leaves strict room for all inequalities and yields the final `619/600` accumulator.

The included `search_chairman_family.py` enumerates small-denominator choices satisfying these inequalities.

## Reproducibility boundary

Exploratory transient notebooks used during the search are not needed for either theorem and are not treated as evidence. The final claims depend only on the explicit data, exact proofs, exhaustive flow enumeration, and independent chairman MILP model committed here.
